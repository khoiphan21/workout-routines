# Strength & Hypertrophy

Evidence on volume, proximity to failure, frequency, and progressive overload for muscle growth and strength.

## Minimum Effective Volume (MEV) vs Maximum Recoverable Volume (MRV)

### Key findings

- **Volume–hypertrophy relationship:** Higher weekly set volume improves hypertrophy, with diminishing returns at higher volumes.
- **Dose-response:** A 2024 meta-regression (Pelland et al.) of 67 studies and 2,058 participants found hypertrophy increases with weekly volume, but gains level off at higher volumes.
- **Practical range:** At least 10 sets per muscle group per week supports hypertrophy. Optimal ranges often cited as roughly 10–20 sets per week; diminishing returns above ~20–25 sets.
- **Volume measurement:** Count “hard sets” (within 0–3 reps of failure) per muscle group per week, excluding warm-up and easy sets.

### Caveats

- MEV and MRV are conceptual; precise thresholds vary by person and muscle group.
- “Fractional” volume—separating direct vs indirect sets for each muscle—predicts adaptations better than raw set counts.

### Implications for this program

- Aim for ~10+ hard sets per major muscle group per week.
- When adding volume, prioritize increments of 2–4 sets per muscle group before adding more.

## Set Proximity to Failure (RIR)

### Key findings

- **Similar hypertrophy:** In trained lifters, training to momentary failure vs 1–2 RIR produced similar quad hypertrophy over 8 weeks.
- **Dose-response:** Hypertrophy tends to improve as sets get closer to failure; very light loads far from failure are likely suboptimal.
- **Trade-offs:** Training to failure increases neuromuscular fatigue; 1–2 RIR allows similar volume accumulation with less repetition loss across sets.
- **Strength:** Strength gains appear similar across a wide range of RIR; hypertrophy may benefit more from proximity to failure than strength does.

### Practical takeaway

- For hypertrophy: 1–2 RIR (or occasional failure on last sets) is usually sufficient.
- For strength: 2–3 RIR often works well while reducing fatigue.
- Avoid routinely training every set to failure; recovery cost can exceed benefit.

### Implications for this program

- Power triples: ~85–90%, ~1 RIR on first 2 sets.
- Hypertrophy: RPE 8–9 (1–2 RIR).

## Training Frequency

### Key findings

- **Volume vs frequency:** Volume has a stronger relationship with hypertrophy than frequency; frequency effects are small or negligible when volume is matched.
- **Minimum frequency:** Training each muscle at least 2× per week tends to outperform 1× per week when weekly volume is similar.
- **Beyond 2×:** Little evidence that 3× or more per week adds meaningful hypertrophy over 2× when total volume is equal.

### Practical takeaway

- Prefer at least 2 sessions per muscle group per week.
- Focus on total weekly volume and distribute it across sessions rather than maximizing frequency.

### Implications for this program

- Push A / Push B and Pull A / Pull B provide 2× frequency for push and pull; this aligns with the evidence.

## Progressive Overload

### Key findings

- Progressive overload is needed for ongoing adaptation.
- Options include: load, reps, sets, tempo, rest, and density.
- For strength: load increases and technique refinement are primary.
- For hypertrophy: volume (sets × reps) and proximity to failure matter more than small load increases.

## Stretch-Mediated Hypertrophy / Lengthened Partials

### Key findings

- **Lengthened vs shortened partials:** Partials performed at long muscle lengths tend to produce more hypertrophy than shortened partials.
- **Lengthened partials vs full ROM:** Evidence is mixed; some analyses suggest a small advantage for lengthened partials (e.g. ~6.76%), but confidence intervals often include no difference.
- **“Stretch-mediated”:** Classic stretch-mediated hypertrophy used extreme protocols; the mechanism of lengthened partials may be passive tension or other factors, not pure stretch.
- **Regional effects:** Long-length training can increase hypertrophy in distal and central regions compared with short-length training.

### Practical takeaway

- Lengthened partials (e.g. bottom half of squat, Romanian deadlift) can be useful for variation and targeting lengthened positions.
- Full ROM remains a solid default; lengthened partials can complement it rather than replace it.
