# Recovery & Adaptation

Evidence on rest intervals, session recovery, sleep, and deload protocols.

## Rest Intervals Between Sets

### Key findings

- **Hypertrophy:** Meta-analyses show only small, inconsistent differences in hypertrophy between short (≤60 s) and long (>60 s) rest.
  - A 2024 Bayesian meta-analysis found overlap between short and long rest; no clear winner.
  - Arms showed a small advantage for longer rests (SMD ~0.13); whole-body marginally favored shorter rests (SMD ~−0.08).
- **Strength:** Rest interval matters more for strength. Longer rest (>2 min) tends to maximize strength in trained individuals.
- **Volume load:** Very short rest can reduce volume per set; ~90 s or more limits most of the volume loss.
- **Untrained individuals:** Can make good strength gains with 60–120 s rest.

### Practical takeaway

- **Strength (3–5 reps):** 3–4 min rest.
- **Hypertrophy (8–15 reps):** 60–90 s is usually enough; slight gains possible with longer rest, but trade-off with session length.
- **Supersets/circuits:** 60 s between exercises is reasonable for hypertrophy.

### Implications for this program

- Power triples: 3–4 min rest (matches evidence).
- Hypertrophy supersets: 60 s between paired exercises (consistent with research).
- Volume week: 60–75 s in supersets is within the supported range.

## Recovery Between Sessions

### Key findings

- Same-muscle recovery depends on volume, intensity, and individual factors.
- High-volume, near-failure work may need 48–72 h for the same muscle group.
- Lower volume or submaximal work can often be repeated sooner (e.g. 24–48 h).

### Practical takeaway

- Avoid heavy work to the same muscle on consecutive days.
- The program’s Push A / Pull A / Conditioning / Push B / Pull B sequence allows adequate recovery between same-muscle sessions.

## Sleep and Performance

### Key findings

- Sleep loss impairs cognitive, metabolic, and hormonal markers linked to recovery.
- Direct meta-analyses on sleep × resistance training are limited, but the broader evidence supports sleep as important for adaptation.
- Recommendations: 7–9 h per night; consistent timing when possible.

### Practical takeaway

- Prioritize sleep during heavy training phases.
- If sleep is poor, consider slightly lower volume or intensity rather than adding more stress.

## Deload Protocols

### Key findings

- **Hypertrophy:** One-week deloads (reduced volume/frequency) generally do not harm muscle growth. Untrained and trained groups show similar hypertrophy with and without deload.
- **Strength:** In trained individuals, continuous training may produce somewhat larger strength gains than a 1-week deload.
- **Power and endurance:** No meaningful difference between deload and continuous training for power or local muscular endurance.
- **“Re-sensitization”:** Strong evidence for anabolic re-sensitization from brief deloads is lacking.

### Practical takeaway

- Deloads are useful for managing fatigue; they do not appear to hinder hypertrophy.
- If strength is the priority, avoid deloads during peak strength phases unless fatigue is clearly high.
- Common practice: ~1 week deload every 4–6 weeks by reducing volume 40–60% and/or intensity.

### Implications for this program

- Progression failure rule (e.g. two misses → deload 7–10%) is consistent with fatigue management.
- Planned deloads (e.g. every 4th week) can be added for high-stress phases.
