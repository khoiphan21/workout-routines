# Periodization & Programming

Evidence on linear vs undulating periodization, concurrent training, warm-ups, and skill acquisition.

## Linear vs Undulating Periodization

### Key findings

- **Strength:** Daily undulating periodization (DUP) may produce slightly greater 1RM gains than linear periodization (LP) in trained individuals (ES ~0.31–0.61).
- **Hypertrophy:** When volume is equated, LP and DUP show similar hypertrophy (e.g. Cohen’s d ~−0.02).
- **Trained vs untrained:** Any advantage for DUP over LP is mainly in trained lifters; differences in untrained groups are small.
- **Body composition:** LP and DUP have similar effects on body composition and metabolic markers; DUP may offer small advantages in some groups (e.g. lean mass in obese individuals).
- **Practical interpretation:** Both models work; DUP can add variation and may slightly favor strength in experienced lifters.

### Practical takeaway

- For hypertrophy-focused programs, LP and DUP are both valid.
- For strength focus, consider DUP or block periodization.
- This program’s week-type structure (Power, Tempo, Volume) is a form of undulation and aligns with this.

### Implications for this program

- Power / Tempo / Volume rotation provides load and rep variation; this is consistent with periodization research.

## Concurrent Training (Strength + Cardio)

### Key findings

- **Interference effect:** Combining resistance and endurance training can reduce strength and hypertrophy gains compared with resistance training alone, but the effect is usually modest.
- **Effect sizes (example):**
  - Strength: resistance only (1.76) > concurrent (1.44) > endurance only (0.78)
  - Hypertrophy: resistance only (1.23) > concurrent (0.85) > endurance only (0.27)
- **Moderators:**
  - **Modality:** Running tends to cause more interference than cycling.
  - **Endurance volume:** Higher frequency and duration of endurance work increase interference.
  - **Session timing:** Interference is often greater when cardio and strength are done in the same session; separating by 3+ hours or different days can help.

### Practical takeaway

- Concurrent training is viable for most goals; interference is real but manageable.
- To minimize interference: prefer cycling over running; keep endurance volume moderate; separate sessions when possible.
- For mainly strength/hypertrophy: treat Conditioning Day as lighter work; avoid high-volume endurance on heavy strength days.

### Implications for this program

- Conditioning Day (circuit, EMOM, L-sit) uses moderate-intensity, calisthenics-style work, which is less likely to cause large interference than heavy running.

## Warm-Up and Activation

### Key findings

- General warm-ups (e.g. light movement, mobility) can improve readiness and movement quality.
- Specific warm-up sets (e.g. lighter loads before working sets) help performance in the main lift.
- “Activation” protocols (e.g. band work, isolation before compounds) may help in some cases but are not essential for everyone.
- Excessive warm-up can add fatigue; balance preparation with conservation of energy.

### Practical takeaway

- Use a short general warm-up (5–10 min).
- Use 1–3 progressively heavier specific warm-up sets before main work.
- The program’s general warm-up (band work, mobility) is consistent with common practice.

## Skill Acquisition (Calisthenics Progressions)

### Key findings

- Motor learning principles apply: practice, feedback, and progressive difficulty support skill development.
- Variability of practice can improve transfer and robustness.
- Rest and recovery matter for skill consolidation.
- Breaking skills into progressions (e.g. tuck → advanced tuck → one-leg front lever) is a standard approach.

### Practical takeaway

- Practice skills when fresh (e.g. early in the session).
- Use clear progressions and regressions.
- Prioritize consistency over maximal intensity in skill work.

### Implications for this program

- Skills (front lever, back lever, handstand, muscle-up) are placed after power and before hypertrophy, which supports skill practice when relatively fresh.
