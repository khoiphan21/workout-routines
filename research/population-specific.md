# Population-Specific Considerations

Evidence on beginners vs intermediates, aging, and sex differences in resistance training.

## Beginners vs Intermediates

### Key findings

- **Volume dose-response:** More volume generally produces more hypertrophy and strength, with diminishing returns at higher volumes.
- **Beginners:** Can progress with lower volumes (e.g. 3–6 sets per muscle group per week); rapid neural and technical adaptations.
- **Intermediates:** Typically need more volume (e.g. 10–20 sets per muscle group per week) for continued hypertrophy; 12–20 sets per week is often cited as an effective range.
- **Diminishing returns:** For strength, diminishing returns are more pronounced than for hypertrophy as volume increases.
- **Direct vs indirect sets:** Classifying “direct” vs “indirect” sets per muscle improves prediction of adaptations; multi-joint exercises contribute to several muscle groups.

### Practical takeaway

- Beginners: Start with 3–6 hard sets per muscle per week; focus on consistency and technique.
- Intermediates: Gradually increase to 10–20 sets per muscle per week; monitor recovery.
- Use “hard sets” (within 0–3 reps of failure) for volume counting; exclude warm-up and easy sets.

### Implications for this program

- The program suits intermediates who can handle the volume and skill demand.
- Beginners can reduce volume (e.g. fewer sets in hypertrophy blocks) and use easier skill progressions.

## Aging and Muscle

### Key findings

- **Sarcopenia:** Muscle mass and strength tend to decline with age; resistance training can attenuate or reverse some of this.
- **Anabolic resistance:** Older adults may need slightly higher protein and possibly more volume or frequency to maximize gains.
- **Recovery:** Recovery can be slower with age; adequate rest, sleep, and nutrition become more important.
- **Adaptability:** Older adults still respond well to resistance training; the principles of overload and progression apply.

### Practical takeaway

- Older trainees: Prioritize consistency; consider slightly longer rest and careful volume progression.
- Ensure sufficient protein (e.g. 1.2–1.6 g/kg) and recovery.
- Full ROM and controlled tempo can support joint health and motor control.

### Implications for this program

- Program structure (power, skills, hypertrophy) can work across ages; adjust volume and rest as needed.
- Calisthenics progressions allow scaling by leverage and assistance.

## Sex Differences

### Key findings

- **Hypertrophy:** Per unit of muscle mass, men and women show similar relative hypertrophy; absolute gains may differ due to baseline size.
- **Recovery:** Some data suggest women may recover slightly faster; individual variation is large.
- **Volume tolerance:** Women may tolerate similar or higher volumes in some contexts; evidence is still growing.
- **Strength:** Absolute strength differs; relative improvements are often similar when accounting for baseline.

### Practical takeaway

- Apply the same principles (progressive overload, volume, recovery) to all sexes.
- Individualize volume and intensity based on response and recovery rather than sex alone.
- The program is designed to be scalable for different strength levels (e.g. partners at different levels).

### Implications for this program

- The program’s structure and scaling options (progressions, assistance, tempo) support use by both men and women.

---

## Training Status Definitions

- **Beginner:** <1 year consistent resistance training; rapid neural and technical gains.
- **Intermediate:** 1–5 years; gains require more volume and structured progression.
- **Advanced:** 5+ years; small margins for improvement; fine-tuning of volume, intensity, and recovery.
