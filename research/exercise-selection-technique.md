# Exercise Selection & Technique

Evidence on compound vs isolation, exercise order, tempo, and range of motion.

## Compound vs Isolation

### Key findings

- **Both contribute:** Compound and isolation exercises can support hypertrophy; neither is strictly superior in all contexts.
- **Compound benefits:** Multi-joint exercises allow higher loads, involve more muscle mass, and can be more efficient per exercise.
- **Isolation benefits:** Single-joint exercises allow focused loading of specific muscles and can help weak points.
- **Volume context:** When total volume is sufficient, adding isolation to compounds can improve development of specific muscles (e.g. arms, lateral delts).

### Practical takeaway

- Base programs on compound lifts (presses, rows, pulls, squats, hinges).
- Add isolation for prioritization or weak points: e.g. lateral raises, curls, triceps work.
- This program’s mix of compounds (dips, pull-ups, bench, rows) plus isolation (lateral raises, triceps) aligns with this approach.

## Exercise Order

### Key findings

- **Hypertrophy:** Pre-exhaustion (single-joint before multi-joint) and traditional order (multi-joint first) produce similar hypertrophy.
- **Strength:** Strength gains are largest when an exercise is performed first in a session.
  - Multi-joint exercises gain more when done first (ES ~0.32 vs later in session).
  - Single-joint exercises gain more when done first (ES ~−0.58).
- **Training efficiency:** Pre-exhaustion can reduce total volume while keeping similar strength and hypertrophy, but traditional order may slightly favor fat loss.
- **Weak-point priority:** Putting a weak exercise first can help if strength in that lift is the main goal.

### Practical takeaway

- **Strength focus:** Perform the main strength exercises first (e.g. power triples).
- **Hypertrophy focus:** Order matters less; pairing exercises in supersets (e.g. agonist–antagonist) can save time.
- Default: power/compound first, then skills, then hypertrophy/isolation.

### Implications for this program

- Power → skills → hypertrophy order is consistent with the evidence and supports both strength and hypertrophy.

## Tempo and Time Under Tension

### Key findings

- Controlled eccentrics can support hypertrophy and reduce injury risk.
- Very slow tempos (e.g. 10 s per rep) do not clearly outperform moderate tempos; they reduce volume load.
- Tempo changes (e.g. 3-0-1, 3-1-3) can introduce variation and technique focus.
- Pauses at the stretched position may add stimulus without large increases in load.

### Practical takeaway

- Use controlled eccentrics (e.g. 2–4 s) as a default.
- Use tempo as a progression or variation tool (e.g. Tempo Week) rather than a constant.
- Avoid extremely slow tempos if volume load and recovery are concerns.

### Implications for this program

- Tempo Week (e.g. 3-0-1 on power, 3-1-3 on hypertrophy) provides variation and is in line with this.

## Range of Motion (ROM)

### Key findings

- **Full vs partial ROM:** Full ROM tends to produce equal or slightly better hypertrophy and strength than partial ROM.
- **Lengthened partials:** Partials at long muscle lengths can outperform shortened partials and may match or slightly exceed full ROM in some analyses, but the evidence is mixed.
- **Muscle regions:** Long-length training can enhance hypertrophy in distal and central regions.
- **Practical interpretation:** Full ROM is a solid default; lengthened partials are a useful variation and may help specific muscle regions.

### Practical takeaway

- Prioritize full ROM where technique and joint health allow.
- Use lengthened partials (e.g. bottom half of squat, RDL) as an occasional variation.
- If injury limits ROM, work within a safe range and progress toward full ROM over time.

### Implications for this program

- Program cues emphasize full ROM and controlled eccentrics, which matches the evidence.
